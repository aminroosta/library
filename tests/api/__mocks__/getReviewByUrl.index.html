Eat, Pray, Love
by Elizabeth Gilbert (Goodreads Author)
Simone Ramone's review
Nov 15, 2009

did not like it
bookshelves: book-club
Read in November, 2009

I found this book unbelievably phoney.

I hated this so much that I got up early this morning to finish it and gave my copy to the library and honestly, I'm not too proud of that.
To me it just felt so insincere that there's no chance I would have made it past the second chapter had it not been for book club obligations.

I enjoyed her writing style, but I absolutely could not warm to her at all. To be fair, I do think she would be an excellent travel writer.

The section on India was agony to read.
I have met enough people freshly returned from Indian ashrams to know that they often seem a tad self absorbed and I also suspect that they really only get up at 3am so that they have even more "me" time.
She didn't do much to alter my opinion.
Honestly, this woman meditated longer, harder and bluer than anyone else has, past or present. She won the meditation competition that no-one was actually having.

Possibly it was not enlightenment that she found, but simply that she finally became completely self absorbed.
Easy mistake to make.

message 6: by Julie (last edited May 06, 2011 05:12PM) (new) - rated it 3 stars

Pretty sure Elizabeth Gilbert did not like herself or her life, which propelled her to leave her life and expose her painful search for something else. Maybe she herself had eaten the sh__ sandwich already, puked it up and you were reacting to the vomit. The story does bring a difficult question forward about the purpose of an authentic self. In one of the cultures she visited, India there are differences of opinion as to whether this 'self' seeking is sensible or meaningful in a Buddhist sense at all. It is described as the question of whethere Atman is real. But underneath all of that is to find an end to suffering, personal, local, international. My take away from that is how does one woman find relief in a world when her own personal situation was so painful. Where was the pain coming from? Herself? Or was there something beyond this? Anyway self-help how-to books in the global range are sure to raise some eyebrows. The book to me was about Gilbert's doubt and her addressing that doubt in a novel way, imperfect and maybe imprecise.
